A1 · Introduction to General Pathology

Pathology = science of disease; studies structural & functional abnormalities and their effects on the body.

Disease Terminology

Doctor's Diagnostic Approach

  • History → symptoms
  • Clinical exam → signs
  • Provisional + DD from signs/symptoms
  • Final Dx after investigations: Lab · Radiology · Pathology
Sequence: Hx → PE → Provisional/DD → Investigations → Final Dx → Prognosis

A2 · Inflammation — Definition & Types

Inflammation: reaction of living vascularised tissue against an irritant to eliminate it or its effects (e.g. toxin). Consists of vascular, lymphatic & cellular changes. Protective process. Suffix "-itis" = inflamed organ (tonsillitis, appendicitis).

Types by Duration

TypeOnset / Duration
AcuteRapid / days–weeks
Sub-acuteBetween acute & chronic
ChronicGradual / months–years

Causes of Inflammation

Living irritants: bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
Non-living: physical (heat, cold, radiation), chemical (acids), mechanical (trauma)
Other: hypoxia · ischaemia → tissue necrosis · immunological reactions

A3 · Acute Inflammation — Vascular & Cellular Events

The acute inflammatory reaction consists of 3 components:

  1. Local tissue damage – central cells killed (necrosis), surrounding cells degenerate. Damaged cells release mediators: histamine, bradykinin, prostaglandins.
  2. Vascular phase – transient vasoconstriction → vasodilation → ↑permeability → stasis.
  3. Cellular exudate formation – margination → adhesion → emigration → chemotaxis → phagocytosis.

Vascular Phase

StepMediator / MechanismEffect
Transient vasoconstrictionDirect irritant on vessel wallFew seconds to minutes
VasodilationHistamine (mainly), bradykinin, PGs↑ blood flow → redness, hotness
↑ Vascular permeabilityHistamine + bradykinin → widening of capillary inter-endothelial poresFluid exudate leakage

Inflammatory Fluid Exudate

How it is formed (4 mechanisms)

  1. ↑ capillary permeability (mainly)
  2. ↑ intravascular hydrostatic pressure (vasodilation)
  3. ↑ extravascular osmotic pressure (protein breakdown)
  4. ↑ fluidity of ground substance
Exudate vs Transudate: Exudate is rich in protein (esp. fibrinogen), high specific gravity. Transudate = low protein, low SG (non-inflammatory, e.g. CHF).

Functions of Exudate

  • Dilutes irritant & toxins
  • Brings antibodies (opsonins, antitoxins, bacteriolysins)
  • Fibrinogen → fibrin: localizes/surrounds irritant, scaffold for phagocytes/repair cells
  • Brings nutrition, removes waste
Fate: drained by lymphatics → may cause lymphangitis / lymphadenitis; if bacteria/toxins reach blood → toxaemia, bacteraemia, septicaemia.

Cellular Phase — 5 Sequential Steps

StepKey Point
1. Margination & adhesionPolymorphs/monocytes leave axial stream (stasis) → adhere to swollen sticky endothelium via integrins
2. EmigrationPseudopods through widened pores (amoeboid). Leukocytes secrete collagenases to cross BM
3. Diapedesis of RBCsPassive, pushed with leukocytes
4. ChemotaxisUnidirectional movement toward irritant along fibrin threads. Mediators: C5a, C3a, LTB4, bacterial & tissue products
5. PhagocytosisIngestion/destruction by neutrophils (early) then macrophages (delayed)

Phagocytosis — 3 Steps

  1. Recognition & attachment: bacteria coated by opsonins (opsonization)
  2. Engulfment: pseudopod extension → phagosome → fuses with lysosome (phagolysosome) → enzyme release
  3. Degradation:
    • O₂-dependent: ROS, mainly H₂O₂
    • O₂-independent: lysosomal enzymes
"NM" rule: Neutrophils first (acute), then Macrophages (delayed, proliferate when PMNs ↓)

A4 · Types of Acute Inflammation

I. Suppurative (Pus-Forming) Inflammation

Acute inflammation with pus formation. Caused by pyogenic bacteria: Staphylococci, Streptococci, Gonococci.

What is Pus?

Thick alkaline fluid; pyogenic bacteria → marked necrosis + excess polymorphs. Killed PMNs = pus cells. Released proteolytic enzymes liquefy necrotic tissue + fibrin → mix with exudate = pus.

Composition of pus: living & dead bacteria + toxins + pigments · liquefied necrotic material (peptones) · fluid exudate · cellular exudate

B1 · Cell Injury — Degenerations

Cell injury occurs when stresses exceed adaptive capacity. Reversible = degeneration (cytoplasmic changes, nucleus intact). Irreversible = necrosis/apoptosis (nuclear damage). Parenchymatous cells with high metabolic rate (liver, heart, kidney) are most susceptible.

Causes of Cell Injury

  • Hypoxia (main): ischaemia (atherosclerosis), inadequate O₂ (cardiopulm. disease), ↓O₂-carrying capacity (anaemia, CO)
  • Physical: trauma, heat, cold, radiation, electric shock
  • Infectious: bacteria, viruses, fungi, rickettsia, parasites
  • Chemical: mercury, arsenic, insecticides, asbestos
  • Immunologic: drugs, autoimmunity
  • Genetic: sickle cell (HbS)
  • Nutritional: ↓protein, ↓vitamins, ↑lipids

Mechanism of Cell Injury (Hypoxia model)

  1. ↓ mitochondrial function → ↓ oxidative phosphorylation → ↓ATP
  2. ↓ Na⁺ pump → K⁺ out, Na⁺ + H₂O in → cell swelling
  3. Interference with protein synthesis
  4. ↓ Ca²⁺ pump → ↑intracellular Ca²⁺ → activates phospholipases (destroy membranes), lysosomal proteases (DNA/protein breakdown)
  5. ↑ Free radicals/ROS → lipid peroxidation, protein/DNA damage (oxidative stress)
"3 Ca²⁺ activators" of cell death: phospholipases, proteases, endonucleases — all activated by ↑intracellular Ca²⁺.

Hydropic Degeneration (Cloudy Swelling)

Mildest, reversible cellular injury — intracellular accumulation of water.

GrossMicro

Classic example: hydropic degeneration of renal tubules in nephritis.

Fatty Change (Steatosis)

Pathological accumulation of neutral fat in parenchymatous cells. Liver most common; also heart, kidney.

Causes (Liver-specific)

  • Hypoxia, toxic effects (general)
  • Excess fats/carbohydrates → obesity
  • Fast mobilization from stores: starvation, DM
  • Liver disease: viral hepatitis
  • Deficiency of lipotropic factors (choline, methionine)
  • Hepatotoxins: alcohol

Pathology

↑ size, weight; tense capsule; soft, yellow, rounded edges, bulging greasy cut surface.
Cells swollen, rounded; cytoplasm contains small fat globules → fuse → single large globule pushing nucleus aside = signet-ring appearance.

B2 · Necrosis & Apoptosis

Necrosis

Irreversible cell injury → death of tissue/cells in living body. Direct or post-degeneration. Cell constituents leak → inflammatory reaction.

Opaque whitish-yellow, surrounded by zone of hyperaemia (inflammation), may be swollen.

Microscopic — Nuclear Changes

ChangeAppearance
PyknosisSmall, irregular, deeply stained nucleus
KaryorrhexisFragmentation of nucleus
KaryolysisFading then disappearance of fragments
Cytoplasm: ↑ eosinophilic (denatured proteins bind eosin) + swollen (cytomegaly). Membrane lost → cells appear as structureless pink "ghost cells". General architecture preserved, details lost. Inflammatory cells infiltrate, macrophages clean up, healing by fibrosis follows.
"PKK" sequence: Pyknosis → Karyorrhexis → Karyolysis.

B3 · Healing (Repair)

Repair = replacement of damaged tissue by healthy new one.

Types of Repair

By RegenerationBy Granulation / Fibrosis
Same type of healthy cells replace damaged onesGranulation tissue matures into fibrous tissue

Types of Cells (By Regenerative Capacity)

TypeCapacityExamples
LabileProliferate continuously; heal by regenerationStratified squamous (skin, mouth, vagina), transitional (urinary), columnar (GIT, resp, endometrium), haemopoietic, lymphoid
StableProliferate only when needed; minor → regeneration, major → fibrosisParenchymatous: liver, pancreas, glands, renal tubules. Mesenchymal: osteoblasts, chondroblasts, smooth muscle
PermanentCannot proliferate → scarCardiac & skeletal muscle, neurons (brain & spinal cord)
"LSP" cells: Labile (always dividing) · Stable (divide on demand) · Permanent (never divide).

Factors Controlling Repair

  • Contact inhibition: adjacent cells inhibit division of each other
  • Chalones: chemical factors from healthy cells inhibiting mitosis nearby — when cells damaged, absent chalones + lack of contact inhibition → neighboring cells proliferate
  • Growth factors: bind cell surface receptors → push from G0→G1; from damaged cells, platelets, macrophages, lymphocytes, fibroblasts
  • Cell–matrix interaction: fibronectin + laminin bind to integrin → cell division/migration/maturation. Defects → abnormal fibrosis

Mechanism of Fibrosis (Healing by Granulation)

  1. Angiogenesis: endothelial migration/proliferation → capillary loops + recruitment of periendothelial cells via VEGF + FGF

  3. ECM secretion: fibroblasts → proteins + collagen I & III
  4. Remodeling/maturation: progressive collagen synthesis, capillary resorption, ↓fibroblasts, ↑collagen, myofibroblast contraction → ↓scar size. Final: pale, avascular scar (dense collagen + few fibroblasts)

C3 · Amyloidosis

Definition: extracellular deposition of abnormal misfolded protein ("amyloid P protein"). Deposited on basement membrane & walls of small blood vessels. Affected tissue becomes hard & waxy.

Pathogenesis

  • Abnormal protein folding → insoluble aggregates → fibrils in extracellular tissues
  • Failure of quality control: normally degraded intracellularly (protease) or extracellularly (macrophages)
  • Two protein categories: (1) Normal proteins prone to misfold when ↑↑ produced; (2) Mutant proteins prone to misfold + aggregate

D1 · Circulatory Disturbances — Hyperaemia & Venous Congestion

Hyperaemia (Active)

Increased blood flow due to active arterial vasodilation.

Mechanism of Thrombosis

  1. Normal endothelium has anti + prothrombotic factors in balance; injury exposes subendothelial collagen
  2. Platelets adhere via von Willebrand factor (VIII) bridging platelet receptors to collagen
  3. Platelet release reaction: ADP, fibrinogen, serotonin secreted; ADP → aggregation (reversible = primary plug)
  4. More ADP + thromboxane → contraction → irreversible aggregation = secondary plug
  5. Platelet factor 3: prothrombin → thrombin → fibrinogen → fibrin → stabilizes plug
  6. Result: mixed thrombus with pink lines of aggregated platelets = lines of Zahn

D5 · Gangrene

Massive tissue necrosis with superimposed putrefaction.

Causes

  • Necrosis: ischaemia or bacterial toxins
  • Putrefaction: saprophytic bacteria → break down proteins → H₂S → black colour (FeS) + foul odour

Types of Gangrene

Dry Gangrene

Limbs with poor collaterals + main artery occlusion. Causes: thrombosis on atherosclerosis, embolism, arterial spasm (Raynaud's, Buerger's, ergot), surgical ligature. Arterial supply cut, venous/lymphatic intact → dryness + evaporation.

Senile Gangrene (Best Example)

  • Old males, atherosclerosis, weak heart, low resistance
  • Starts at big toe (spontaneous or tight shoes)
  • Pale & cold → mummified (shrunken)
  • Saprophytes → black & offensive
  • Toxic products → surrounding thrombosis → spread proximally slowly

Avoiding immune surveillanceTumour Ags via MHC I → CD8⁺ CTLs. Tumours evade by: ↓MHC expression, secreting TGF-β (immunosuppressive). Immunosuppressed patients → ↑risk (esp. DNA virus cancers)

INFLAMMATION — SIGNS & PHASES
5 Signs: Rubor · Tumor · Calor · Dolor · Functio laesa
Vascular: transient VC → VD → ↑permeability → stasis
Cellular: margination → rolling (selectins) → adhesion (integrins/ICAM) → diapedesis → chemotaxis (C5a, LTB4, IL-8) → phagocytosis
Exudate: protein >3 g/dL, SG >1.020 vs Transudate (opposite).
MEDIATORS — pain/fever/VD
Pain: bradykinin, PGE2
Fever: IL-1, TNF, PGE2
VD: histamine, NO, PGI2
VC: TXA2, leukotrienes (early).
ACUTE INFLAMMATION TYPES
Serous (blister, effusion) · Fibrinous = bread-and-butter pericarditis · Suppurative (abscess; Staph/Strep) · Membranous (diphtheria, pseudomembranous colitis) · Haemorrhagic · Catarrhal · Ulcerative.
GRANULOMA
= epithelioid macrophages ± Langhans giant cells ± lymphocyte rim.
Caseating: TB, fungal
Non-caseating: sarcoidosis, leprosy, Crohn's, syphilis (gumma), FB.
NECROSIS vs APOPTOSIS
Necrosis: groups · swelling · membrane ruptured · inflammation YES
Apoptosis: single cell · shrinkage · membrane intact · caspase-driven · NO inflammation
Intrinsic = BCL2 / cyt-c · Extrinsic = Fas / TNF.
NECROSIS TYPES
Coagulative — ischaemia, all organs EXCEPT brain
Liquefactive — brain, abscess
Caseous — TB, fungal, gumma
Fat — pancreatitis (saponification)
Fibrinoid — vasculitis, malignant HTN
Gangrenous — dry/wet/gas
HEALING — TIMELINE
1°: clot → granulation (3–5 d) → scar (minimal)
2°: larger granulation + myofibroblast contraction → big scar
Tensile: 10% @ 1 wk → 70–80% @ 3 mo (never 100%)
↓ Healing: infection, DM, ischaemia, steroids, vit C/Zn def, irradiation.
HEALING COMPLICATIONS
Keloid = beyond margin (sternum/earlobe)
Hypertrophic scar = within margin (regresses)
Contracture (burns, joints)
Marjolin ulcer = SCC in old burn scar
Dehiscence · delayed healing.
ACCUMULATIONS & PIGMENTS
Fatty liver: alcohol, DM, obesity, CCl₄, kwashiorkor
Lipid storage: Gaucher, Niemann-Pick, Tay-Sachs
Pigments: melanin (Addison/melanoma) · lipofuscin (aging) · haemosiderin (Prussian blue +) · bilirubin · haematin (malaria) · carbon (anthracosis) · asbestos.
CALCIFICATION
Dystrophic (normal Ca²⁺, damaged tissue): atherosclerosis, old TB, psammoma bodies = papillary thyroid · serous ovary · meningioma
Metastatic (↑ Ca²⁺, normal tissue): HPT, vit D toxicity, MM, milk-alkali → lung/kidney/stomach.
AMYLOIDOSIS
β-pleated misfolded protein → Congo red + → apple-green birefringence (polarised light).
AL = myeloma · AA = chronic inflam (RA/TB) · Aβ = Alzheimer · ATTR = senile cardiac · β2M = dialysis · A-Cal = medullary thyroid.
VIRCHOW'S TRIAD
(1) Endothelial injury · (2) Stasis · (3) Hypercoagulability (Factor V Leiden, prot C/S def, OCPs, malignancy).
Lines of Zahn = ante-mortem thrombus.
EMBOLISM
PE: 95% from leg DVT · saddle embolus = fatal
Systemic: 80% from LV mural
Fat (long bone #) · Air (Caisson) · Amniotic (labour → DIC) · Septic (IE) · Tumour.
INFARCTION — RED vs WHITE
Red (loose tissue / dual supply / venous): lung · intestine · testis · ovary
White (end-artery / solid): heart · kidney · spleen
Brain = exception → liquefactive necrosis
GANGRENE
Dry — arterial; dry/black/mummified, clear demarcation (DM foot, Buerger)
Wet — venous + infection; foul, no demarcation (bowel)
Gas — Clostridium perfringens; crepitus; emergency.
ADAPTATIONS
Atrophy ↓size · Hypertrophy ↑size (LVH) · Hyperplasia ↑number (BPH, endometrium)
Metaplasia reversible (Barrett's: squamous→columnar; bronchus: columnar→squamous in smokers)
Dysplasia = pre-cancer (reversible if mild)
CIS = full-thickness, BM intact.
BENIGN vs MALIGNANT
Benign: slow · encapsulated · well-diff · no metastasis · "-oma"
Malignant: infiltrative · anaplastic · ↑N:C ratio · pleomorphism · metastasis
Carcinoma → lymphatic spread · Sarcoma → blood spread, radioresistant.
CARCINOGENESIS — KEY GENES
Oncogenes: RAS (colon/lung), MYC = Burkitt t(8;14), ERBB2/HER2 (breast), ABL = CML t(9;22), BCL2 = follicular t(14;18)
Suppressors: RB = "governor" (retinoblastoma), TP53 = "guardian" (Li-Fraumeni), APC (FAP), BRCA1/2.
TUMOUR MARKERS & IHC
AFP–HCC · CEA–colon · PSA–prostate · CA-125–ovary · CA 19-9–pancreas · β-hCG–choriocarcinoma · calcitonin–medullary thyroid.
IHC: LCA=lymphoma · CK=carcinoma · Vimentin=sarcoma · S100=melanoma/Schwann.
Stage > Grade for prognosis (TNM).
